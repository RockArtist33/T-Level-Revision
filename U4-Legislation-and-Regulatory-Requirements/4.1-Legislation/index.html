    # 4.1.1 - Health and safety when working with computers
    • Display screen regulations

      • The Health and Safety (Display Screen Equipment) Regulations 1992 is the main piece of UK legislation that covers the use of display screen equipment, including computers, in the workplace. These regulations require employers to take steps to protect the health and safety of employees who use display screen equipment as part of their job.

      • Under the regulations, employers must conduct a risk assessment to identify any potential health and safety risks associated with the use of display screen equipment, and put in place measures to address these risks. This may include providing employees with regular breaks, training on how to use the equipment properly, and ensuring that the equipment is set up ergonomically. Employers must also provide employees with information and training on the health and safety risks associated with the use of display screen equipment, and how these risks can be reduced.

      • In addition to the Health and Safety (Display Screen Equipment) Regulations, employers must also comply with other health and safety legislation, such as the Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 and the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999. These laws require employers to take steps to protect the health and safety of employees in the workplace, including those who use display screen equipment.

    • General working environment

      1. Employers must provide a safe and healthy working environment for employees who use computers.

      2. Employers must ensure that employees have access to ergonomic equipment, including adjustable chairs and desks, to prevent musculoskeletal disorders.

      3. Employers must provide adequate training on how to use computers safely and effectively, including proper posture and ergonomic techniques.

      4. Employers must conduct regular assessments of the working environment to identify and address any potential health and safety risks associated with computer use.

      5. Employers must provide employees with regular breaks from computer use to prevent fatigue and eyestrain.

      6. Employers must provide appropriate lighting and ventilation in the work area to prevent headaches and other health issues.

      7. Employers must ensure that employees have access to appropriate eye care, including regular vision tests and protective eyewear if necessary.

      8. Employers must provide adequate security measures to protect employees from cyber threats and data breaches.

      9. Employers must follow all relevant health and safety regulations and guidelines, including those related to fire safety and emergency evacuation procedures.

      10. Employers must report any incidents or accidents involving computer use to the appropriate authorities and take appropriate corrective action to prevent future occurrences.

    • Possible risks and prevention

      1. Repetitive strain injury (RSI) - avoid prolonged and repetitive use of the keyboard and mouse, take regular breaks, adjust chair and desk height to a comfortable and ergonomic position.

      2. Eye strain - reduce glare on the screen, adjust screen brightness and contrast, take regular breaks to look away from the screen.

      6. Cybersecurity - use antivirus and firewall software to protect against malware and other security threats, do not open suspicious emails or attachments, regularly update software to ensure security patches are applied.

    # 4.1.2 - Data Protection Act

    • The principles of the act

        1. The data must be collected for a specific and lawful purpose.

      1. The data must be accurate and up-to-date.

      2. The data must be kept for no longer than necessary.

      3. The data must be processed fairly and transparently.

      4. The data must be kept secure.

      5. The data must be kept in a way that allows the subject to access and correct their own data.

      6. The data must not be transferred outside the European Economic Area without adequate protection.

    • General Data Protection Regulations (GDPR)

      • The General Data Protection Regulations (GDPR) is a set of rules and regulations that were introduced in the European Union (EU) in 2018. The GDPR strengthens and expands the rights of individuals with regard to their personal data and how it is collected, used, and shared by organizations.
      • The GDPR applies to all organizations operating in the EU, as well as organizations outside the EU that collect, process, or use the personal data of individuals in the EU. The GDPR sets out strict rules for the handling of personal data, including the need for clear and explicit consent from individuals for the collection and use of their personal data.
      • The GDPR also requires organizations to implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to protect personal data from unauthorized access, disclosure, or loss. Organizations that violate the GDPR can face significant fines and penalties.

    • Marketing consent

      • Under the UK Data Protection Act, organizations are required to obtain the explicit consent of individuals before collecting and using their personal data for marketing purposes. This means that organizations must clearly explain how the personal data will be used and obtain the individual’s explicit agreement to this use.
      • Organizations must also provide individuals with the opportunity to withdraw their consent at any time, and must respect their decision to do so. In addition, organizations must ensure that personal data is collected and used in a fair and transparent manner, and must implement appropriate measures to protect the personal data from unauthorized access, disclosure, or loss.

    • The rights of the data subject

      • Under the UK Data Protection Act, individuals have several rights with regard to their personal data. These include the right to:

      1. Access their personal data - individuals have the right to request a copy of the personal data that an organization holds about them, and to be informed of the purposes for which it is being processed.

      2. Rectification - individuals have the right to request that any inaccurate or incomplete personal data be corrected.

      3. Erasure - individuals have the right to request that their personal data be erased, unless there is a compelling reason for it to be retained.

      4. Restriction of processing - individuals have the right to request that the processing of their personal data be restricted, for example if they contest the accuracy of the data.

      5. Object to processing - individuals have the right to object to the processing of their personal data, for example if they believe it is being used for a purpose other than that for which it was originally collected.

      6. Data portability - individuals have the right to request that their personal data be transferred to another organization.

      7. Complain to the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO) - individuals have the right to make a complaint to the ICO if they believe that an organization is not complying with the Data Protection Act.

    • Enforcement

      • Under the UK Data Protection Act, the enforcement of data protection rights and obligations is carried out by the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO). The ICO is an independent public body that is responsible for upholding information rights in the public interest.

        If an individual believes that an organization is not complying with the Data Protection Act, they have the right to make a complaint to the ICO. The ICO will investigate the complaint and, if necessary, take action against the organization. This may include issuing an enforcement notice, requiring the organization to take specific steps to comply with the Act, or imposing a fine.

        In addition, organizations that fail to comply with the Data Protection Act may face legal action from individuals who have suffered damage as a result of the non-compliance.

    # 4.1.3 - Computer Misuse Act

    • The principles of the act

      • The Computer Misuse Act is a UK law that was introduced in 1990 to criminalize certain types of unauthorized access to computer systems. The Act has three main principles.

      1. Unauthorized access to computer systems - it is an offense to intentionally access a computer system without authorization, or to exceed authorized access. This includes hacking into a computer system, using someone else’s login credentials, or accessing a system for which access has been denied.

      2. Unauthorized modification of computer material - it is an offense to intentionally or recklessly modify computer material without authorization. This includes deleting or altering files, or introducing malware into a computer system.

      3. Unauthorized use of computer systems - it is an offense to intentionally or recklessly cause a computer to perform any function with intent to secure access to any program or data held in a computer, or to enable any such access to be secured. This includes using a computer to commit a crime, such as fraud or theft.

        • The Act also sets out penalties for offenses under the Act, including imprisonment and fines.

    • Consequences (company and employee)

      • Employees

        • For the individual employee, the consequences may include:

        1. Imprisonment - the individual may be sentenced to imprisonment if found guilty of an offense under the Act.

        2. Fines - the individual may be required to pay a fine if found guilty of an offense under the Act.

        3. Loss of employment - the individual may lose their job if their employer takes disciplinary action as a result of the offense.

        4. Damage to reputation - the individual may suffer damage to their reputation if they are publicly associated with a breach of the Act. This can affect their future job prospects and ability to find employment.

      • Employer

        • For the organization, the consequences may include:

        1. Financial penalties - the organization may be required to pay a fine if found guilty of an offense under the Act.

        2. Reputational damage - the organization may suffer damage to its reputation if it is publicly associated with a breach of the Act. This can lead to a loss of trust and confidence from customers and other stakeholders.

        3. Loss of contracts - the organization may lose contracts with customers or other organizations if they are unwilling to do business with a company that has breached the Act.

    • Employee awareness.

      • Employees should be aware of the Computer Misuse Act and their obligations under the Act. This includes:

      1. Not accessing computer systems without authorization, or exceeding authorized access.

      2. Not modifying computer material without authorization.

      3. Not causing a computer to perform any function with intent to secure unauthorized access to programs or data.

      4. Not using a computer to commit a crime.

        Employees should also be aware of the consequences of violating the Act, including potential imprisonment and fines. They should report any suspected violations to their manager or the appropriate authority, and cooperate with any investigations into such violations.

    # 4.1.4 - Equality Act

    • Types of discrimination (protected characteristics)

      1. Age - treating someone differently because of their age.

      2. Disability - treating someone differently because of a physical or mental impairment that has a substantial and long-term adverse effect on their ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities.

      3. Gender reassignment - treating someone differently because they are undergoing, have undergone, or are planning to undergo a process to reassign their sex.

      4. Marriage and civil partnership - treating someone differently because they are married or in a civil partnership.

      5. Pregnancy and maternity - treating a woman unfavorably because she is pregnant or has recently given birth.

      6. Race - treating someone differently because of their race, color, nationality, or ethnic or national origin.

      7. Religion or belief - treating someone differently because of their religion, beliefs, or lack of belief.

      8. Sex - treating someone differently because of their gender.

      9. Sexual orientation - treating someone differently because of their sexual orientation.

    • Where individuals are protected

      • The Equality Act protects individuals from discrimination in various areas of their lives, including:

      1. Employment - the Act protects individuals from discrimination in the workplace, including in relation to hiring, promotion, pay, and benefits.

      2. Education - the Act protects individuals from discrimination in education, including in relation to admissions, access to facilities, and the provision of services.

      3. Services, public functions, and associations - the Act protects individuals from discrimination in relation to the provision of goods, facilities, and services, and in relation to the carrying out of public functions. It also protects individuals from discrimination by associations, such as clubs and other organizations.

      4. Transport - the Act protects individuals from discrimination in relation to transport services.

      5. Premises - the Act protects individuals from discrimination in relation to the use of premises, such as shops, restaurants, and other public places.

      6. Housing - the Act protects individuals from discrimination in relation to the provision of housing, including in relation to renting or buying a property.

        Overall, the this means that individuals should not be treated unfairly because of their age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage or civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, sexual orientation, or gender reassignment.

    • When to take action against discrimination.

      • Individuals should take action against discrimination when they believe that they have been treated unfairly because of a protected characteristic, such as their age, disability, gender, race, or sexual orientation.
      • If an individual believes that they have been the victim of discrimination, they should first raise the issue with the person or organization that they believe is responsible. If the issue is not resolved, the individual may wish to consider seeking advice from an advocacy group or legal professional, or making a complaint to the relevant regulatory body or court.

    • How individuals can be discriminated against (direct, Indirect, harassment and victimisation).

      1. Direct discrimination - treating someone less favorably because of a protected characteristic. For example, refusing to hire someone because of their race.

      2. Indirect discrimination - putting in place a policy or practice that disproportionately disadvantages people with a protected characteristic. For example, setting a minimum height requirement for a job that disproportionately affects women.

      3. Harassment - unwanted conduct that is related to a protected characteristic and that has the purpose or effect of violating someone’s dignity or creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, or offensive environment. For example, using racist language towards someone.

      4. Victimisation - treating someone unfairly because they have made a complaint or raised concerns about discrimination. For example, punishing an employee who has made a complaint about sexual harassment.

    # 4.1.5 - Intellectual Property Act
    • Unregistered designs
      • unregistered designs are protected for a period of three years from the date on which the design was first made available to the public. During this period, the owner of the unregistered design has the exclusive right to use, license, or sell the design.
      • If someone uses, licenses, or sells an unregistered design without the permission of the owner, they may be infringing on the owner’s rights. The owner can take legal action to stop the infringement and seek compensation for any losses they have suffered as a result.
      • In order to provide additional protection for their unregistered designs, individuals can apply to register their designs with the IPO. Registered designs are protected for a period of up to 25 years.
    • Registered designs
      • Registered designs are original designs that have been registered with the Intellectual Property Office (IPO) under the Intellectual Property Act. In order to register a design, the applicant must provide the IPO with details of the design, including drawings or photographs, and pay a fee.
      • Once a design is registered, the owner has the exclusive right to use, license, or sell the design for a period of up to 25 years. The registration also provides evidence that the design is original and can be used in legal proceedings to stop others from using the design without permission.
      • In order to maintain the registration, the owner must pay renewal fees at regular intervals. If the owner does not pay the renewal fees, the registration will expire and the design will no longer be protected.
    • Patents
      • A patent is a legal right that is granted to an inventor or creator to prevent others from making, using, selling, or importing an invention without permission. In the UK, patents are granted by the Intellectual Property Office (IPO) under the Intellectual Property Act.
      • In order to obtain a patent, the inventor or creator must apply to the IPO and provide detailed information about the invention, including how it works and how it is different from existing inventions. The IPO will review the application and, if the invention meets the necessary criteria, will grant a patent for a period of up to 20 years.
      • During this period, the owner of the patent has the exclusive right to use, license, or sell the invention. If someone else uses, licenses, or sells the invention without permission, they may be infringing on the patent and the owner can take legal action to stop the infringement and seek compensation for any losses they have suffered.

This can help to protect individuals and organizations in the UK from harm and ensure that they are able to use digital systems without fear of unfair treatment or abuse.

    Overall, the role of legislation relating to international law is essential for the design, development, and use of digital systems in the UK, and is an important part of ensuring that these systems are used in a way that is fair, safe, and compliant with legal requirements in the country.
