    # 7.4.1 - Understand the concepts of cloud computing deployment

    Cloud computing is a model for delivering computing services and resources over the Internet. In cloud computing, the applications, data, runtime, middleware, operating system, virtualization, servers, storage, and networking are all provided and managed by a third-party provider, and they are accessed and used by the organization over the Internet.

    • The applications in cloud computing refer to the software programs and tools that are provided and used by the organization. These applications can be accessed and run on the cloud provider’s infrastructure, and they can be scaled and customized to meet the organization’s needs.

    • The data in cloud computing refers to the information and data that is stored and managed by the cloud provider. This data can be accessed and used by the organization’s applications, and it can be backed up and protected by the cloud provider.

    • The runtime in cloud computing refers to the environment in which the organization’s applications are executed. The runtime can include the operating system, middleware, and other components that are necessary for the applications to run.

    • The middleware in cloud computing refers to the software that is used to connect and integrate the organization’s applications with the cloud provider’s infrastructure. This middleware can provide services such as messaging, security, and data management, and it can help to enable the organization’s applications to access and use the cloud provider’s resources.

    • The operating system in cloud computing refers to the underlying platform that is used to run the organization’s applications.

    • Virtualization is the process of creating and running virtual versions of computer hardware and software resources. This allows multiple virtual environments to be run on the same physical hardware, providing benefits such as increased flexibility and cost-efficiency.

    • Servers are powerful computers that are used to host and provide services to other devices on a network. In a cloud computing deployment, servers are typically used to host applications, store data, and provide other services to users over the Internet.

    • Storage refers to the devices and systems that are used to store data and information. In a cloud computing deployment, storage is typically provided through a network of remote servers, allowing users to access and store data remotely over the Internet.

    • Networking refers to the devices and systems that are used to connect devices and transmit data over a network. In a cloud computing deployment, networking is typically provided through a combination of physical and virtual networks, allowing users to access and use cloud services from anywhere.

    Overall, cloud computing deployment involves the use of virtualization, servers, storage, and networking technologies to provide services and resources to users over the Internet. This allows for a scalable and flexible computing environment that can be accessed and used by users from anywhere.

    # 7.4.2 - Understand common cloud delivery models and the way in which responsibility and ownership of resources are distributed between the subscriber and service provider
    There are several different cloud delivery models, each of which has its own characteristics and defines the way in which responsibility and ownership of resources are distributed between the subscriber and the service provider.

    The three most common cloud delivery models are:

    • Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS): In this model, the service provider provides the underlying infrastructure, such as servers, storage, and networking, and the subscriber is responsible for managing and configuring the operating system, applications, and data. This model provides the greatest flexibility and control to the subscriber, but it also requires the most technical expertise.
    • Platform as a Service (PaaS): In this model, the service provider provides a platform, such as an operating system or development environment, and the subscriber is responsible for managing and configuring the applications and data. This model provides a pre-configured and managed platform, but it may have less flexibility and control than the IaaS model.
    • Software as a Service (SaaS): In this model, the service provider provides a complete application or service, and the subscriber is responsible for using the application or service. This model provides the least amount of flexibility and control to the subscriber, but it requires the least technical expertise.

    In general, the distribution of responsibility and ownership of resources in cloud delivery models depends on the specific model and the agreement between the subscriber and the service provider. The subscriber typically has more control and responsibility in the IaaS and PaaS models, while the service provider has more control and responsibility in the SaaS model.

    # 7.4.3 - Understand the concept of DaaS (Data as a Service)

    Data as a Service (DaaS) is a model for providing data and analytics services to users over the Internet. In a DaaS model, the service provider manages and maintains a platform that includes various data science tools and technologies, and the subscriber can access and use the platform to perform data analytics and other tasks.

    Some of the tools and technologies that may be included in a DaaS platform are:

    • Data science platforms: These are platforms that provide tools and technologies for data analysis, machine learning, and other advanced analytics tasks. Examples include Jupyter Notebooks and RStudio.

    • Dashboards: These are interactive visualizations that allow users to monitor and analyze data in real-time. Examples include Tableau and PowerBI.

    • Business intelligence tools: These are tools that allow users to analyze and report on business data. Examples include Cognos and Qlik.

    • Data lakes: These are large repositories of structured and unstructured data that can be accessed and queried for analytics purposes. Examples include Amazon S3 and Google Cloud Storage.

    • Databases: These are systems that are used to store and manage structured data. Examples include MySQL and SQL Server.

    • File systems: These are systems that are used to store and manage unstructured data. Examples include HDFS and S3.

    Overall, DaaS provides a way for organizations to access and use advanced data analytics tools and technologies without having to manage and maintain the underlying infrastructure. This can provide benefits such as cost-efficiency, flexibility, and scalability.

    # 7.4.4 - Understand how DaaS (Data as a Service) is used by organisations, and the benefits and drawbacks it provides for organisations and their stakeholders.
    DaaS (Data as a Service) is used by organizations to access and use advanced data analytics tools and technologies without having to manage and maintain the underlying infrastructure. This can provide various benefits for organizations and their stakeholders, such as:

    PROS / CONS

    • Cost-efficiency: DaaS can provide a more cost-effective solution for organizations that do not have the resources or expertise to manage and maintain their own data analytics infrastructure.

    • Flexibility: DaaS allows organizations to scale up or down their data analytics capabilities as needed, providing greater flexibility and agility.

    • Expertise: DaaS providers typically have a team of experts who can provide support and guidance to organizations using the platform. This can help organizations to maximize the value of their data analytics efforts.

    • Security: DaaS providers typically have robust security measures in place to protect the data and analytics platform. This can provide peace of mind to organizations and their stakeholders.

    However, there are also some drawbacks to using DaaS, such as:

    • Dependency: Organizations that use DaaS are dependent on the provider for the availability and performance of the platform. If the provider experiences any issues, it could affect the organization’s ability to access and use the data analytics tools and technologies.

    • Control: Organizations that use DaaS have less control over the underlying infrastructure and technology stack. This can limit the organization’s ability to customize and configure the platform to meet their specific needs.

    • Cost: Although DaaS can provide cost-efficiency in some cases, it may also require a significant upfront investment or ongoing subscription fees. This can be a drawback for organizations with limited budgets.

    • Privacy: Organizations that use DaaS may need to share their data with the provider in order to use the platform. This can raise concerns about data privacy and security, and organizations may need to carefully consider their data governance policies and practices.

    Overall, DaaS can provide a range of benefits and drawbacks for organizations and their stakeholders. Organizations should carefully consider their specific needs and requirements, and evaluate the potential costs and risks, before deciding whether DaaS is the right solution for them.

    # 7.4.5 - Understand the concept of cloud sourcing and cloud portability, and the implications for service providers and organisations (subscribers).

    Data as a Service (DaaS) is used by subscribers to access and use advanced data analytics tools and technologies without having to manage and maintain the underlying infrastructure. Subscribers can use a DaaS platform to perform various data analytics tasks, such as data cleaning, analysis, visualization, and machine learning.

    PROS / CONS

    The benefits of DaaS for subscribers include:

    • Cost-efficiency: DaaS can provide access to advanced data analytics tools and technologies at a lower cost than purchasing and maintaining the tools and technologies on-premises.
    • Flexibility: DaaS allows subscribers to scale their usage of data analytics tools and technologies up or down as needed, without having to invest in additional infrastructure.
    • Expertise: DaaS providers often have expertise in data analytics and can provide support and guidance to subscribers.

    The drawbacks of DaaS for subscribers include:

    • Dependency: Subscribers may become dependent on the DaaS provider and may not have as much control over the data analytics tools and technologies as they would if they were managing the tools and technologies on-premises.
    • Security: Subscribers may be concerned about the security of their data when it is stored and processed on a DaaS platform.
    • Integration: DaaS platforms may not be integrated with other systems and tools that the subscriber uses, which may limit the functionality and usability of the platform.

    The implications of DaaS for service providers and organizations are:

    • Service providers: Service providers who offer DaaS must invest in the infrastructure, tools, and expertise to provide the service, and they must manage and maintain the platform. They must also ensure the security and availability of the platform and the data on it.

    • Organizations: Organizations that use DaaS must consider the costs, benefits, and drawbacks of the service, and they must ensure that the service meets their data analytics needs and complies with any security and regulatory requirements. They must also manage their relationship with the DaaS provider and ensure that the service is integrated with their other systems and tools.
